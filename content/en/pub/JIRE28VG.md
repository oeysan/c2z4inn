    <h1>About</h1>
    Čermáková et al. present a book that explores how different languages express and perceive time, comparing Czech, French, German, Mandarin, Norwegian, and Swedish with English. While there's plenty of research on time in language, this book focuses on comparing these languages and examining how they evolve over time. The authors tackle challenges like dealing with low-quality historical data and identifying common phrases (n-grams) across different language types. Their findings highlight how studying multiple languages together can reveal unique language features and uncover cultural and linguistic similarities and differences. This research is important because it helps us understand how language shapes our perception of time and how languages change, offering insights into cultural diversity and communication.

    <h1>Scientific abstract</h1>
    This volume comprises a collection of contrastive studies on language and time. Languages represented include Czech, French, German, Mandarin, Norwegian and Swedish, all of which are contrasted with English. While the amount of published research on temporal relations in general is considerable, less work has been carried out on comparing how we talk about time in various languages and how languages change over time. Several methodological challenges are addressed and solutions proposed, such as how to deal with poor quality historical data and how to identify n-grams in typologically different languages for purposes of comparison. The results of the various studies show how multilingual corpora can increase our knowledge of language-specific features as well as linguistic, typological and cultural differences and similarities across languages.
